📊 Funnel Analysis & Insights — Backend Engineering · Last quarter
01 — Process health
What the data actually says

Two metrics are healthy on the surface. Two are not.

12d

65%
Candidate satisfaction ⚠

Time-to-hire (45 days) sits within the ~6-week industry benchmark. Time-to-fill (72 days) is acceptable once you factor in an average 4-week notice period — the role is effectively live 10 weeks after offer acceptance, which is realistic for senior engineers.

12 days to first HM interview is too long. Senior engineers are in multiple processes at once. Every day you wait, another company moves faster. This is where you lose people before the funnel has really started.

65% candidate satisfaction is a brand problem, not just a process problem. Engineers talk. A poor experience doesn't stay private — it ends up on Glassdoor, in Slack groups, in hallway conversations at meetups.

The final stages are solid — candidates who make it to the end are good and they accept. The problem is everything before that.

  • Recruiter Screen → HM (40%): Only 40 out of 100 screened candidates advance. That means the recruiter is letting through profiles the HM doesn't want. Classic misalignment at intake — dealbreakers weren't clearly defined, or they only surfaced during the call.
  • HM Interview → Technical (50%): The hiring manager is spending time on 40 interviews and rejecting half. That's expensive. The same root cause: the bar going in wasn't sharp enough.
  • Technical Interview → Final (25%): This is the loudest alarm. If 75% fail here, technical fit is being tested way too late. Basic technical screening should happen at recruiter screen level — not as a full assessment, but enough to disqualify obvious mismatches early.
03 — Key insights
Three things that explain it all
01
Recruiter ↔ HM misalignment

Low conversion at both recruiter screen and HM interview point to the same root cause: no shared definition of what good looks like. Fix it at intake, not mid-search.

02
Technical screening too late

25% pass rate at technical round means unqualified candidates are burning everyone's time for three stages before getting caught. Move the check earlier.

03
Slow momentum = poor experience

The 12-day wait and 65% satisfaction score are connected. Candidates feel ignored. In a competitive market, that feeling drives them to whoever responds faster.

04 — Recommendations
What to do about it
Quick wins
  • Cut time-to-first HM interview to ≤5 days — block a recurring weekly slot
  • Build a structured intake checklist with must-haves, dealbreakers, and level criteria. Review after every 5–10 screens.
  • Add 2–3 technical questions to the recruiter screen — not a full assessment, just enough to catch obvious mismatches early
  • Personal rejection calls for every candidate who reached recruiter screen or beyond — 5 minutes, honest feedback, followed by a written email with the same message. We did this at FLYR and it made a measurable difference. Candidates remember how you treated them when it didn't work out.
Longer term
  • Implement a structured pre-screen before the HM interview — async or standardised, validated with engineering
  • Run candidate experience interviews — understand why satisfaction is at 65% and fix the specific moments that hurt
  • Bi-weekly recruiter/HM calibration sessions — review recent screens together, align on what passed and why

"Every candidate who made it to recruiter screen or further gets a personal call — 5 minutes, real feedback, then a follow-up email. It's a small investment with a big return on employer brand."

📋 When the Ground Keeps Moving — Two difficult HMs + a VP update
01 — Real example
When the budget moved mid-search

At FLYR, I was four weeks into sourcing a Senior Backend Engineer in Barcelona. Clear intake, strong pipeline, candidates in multiple interview stages. Then the salary dropped — from €70–75K to a max of €58K. The HM still wanted 7+ years of experience. The role had effectively become a mid-level position with a senior requirement.

First step: understand why. Budget constraints. The HM also genuinely believed €58K was market rate for senior engineers in Barcelona. It isn't.

I shared my concern directly — we had strong profiles who would likely disengage at this number. But staying positive is a choice. I didn't get stuck on what changed. I reviewed the top candidates with the HM and personally reached out to gauge appetite. Several stayed in — they saw the bigger picture at FLYR, the growth potential, what the role could mean for their career.

"We take the setbacks and look for what is possible — without losing too much time."

What I learned: Always validate salary budget with additional stakeholders — finance, direct manager — early. HMs are not always close to the numbers. I also started anchoring candidate salary expectations slightly below our stated maximum during recruiter screens. Buffer for exactly this kind of situation.

02 — The two conversations
Different problems. Different approach.
Hiring Manager 1
Shifting requirements

The irrelevant pipeline is something I can handle on my side. What I make very clear is that this is also his problem. He just lost three weeks. His deadline has slipped. His team is still understaffed. The pressure to explain the delay is now on him.

I make that cause-and-effect explicit — not as blame, but as business reality. Once that lands, we move to solution mode. No more verbal realignment mid-search.

The outcome I'm driving toward: a locked, written role brief we both sign off on. Level, must-haves, dealbreakers, what good looks like at each stage. Then we start again — fast.

Goal: Signed-off brief + reset timeline agreed
Hiring Manager 2
No feedback

I lead with empathy. She's frustrated, she's busy, and she feels the pipeline has dried up. She needs to feel heard before I walk her through what actually happened.

Then I show the data: two strong candidates accepted other offers while waiting. Not as an accusation — as evidence of what slow turnaround costs. Most HMs don't see this connection until you show it.

I walk her through our communication process — Greenhouse tags, 48-hour SLA — not as a lecture but as a practical guide. And I explore a pragmatic option: is there someone on her team she trusts to give feedback when she can't?

Goal: Feedback loop restored + bi-weekly 15-min syncs agreed
03 — VP Engineering update
Lead with context, not excuses

The dashboard shows all six active requisitions — full funnel visibility, plus outreach data from LinkedIn Recruiter: messages sent, response rates, conversion at each stage. The numbers look bad on the surface. My job is to give them the story behind the numbers.

  • HM1: Pipeline reset explained, root cause named, new brief shown, week-one outreach numbers presented. Story: we lost three weeks, we know exactly why, here's what we've done and what the next two weeks look like.
  • HM2: Funnel drop shown, two lost candidates called out with timeline. Followed immediately by the fix — feedback loop restored, bi-weekly syncs in place. Story: bottleneck identified, addressed, now being measured.

"The VP doesn't want to hear about problems. He wants to see that recruiting has situational awareness, takes ownership, and has a plan."

I close with a two-week forecast per role. Not promises — targets backed by pipeline data. Honest, proactive, and in control of the narrative.

🤝 Candidate Attraction — Senior PHP Engineer (Evergreen)
01 — Before anything else
Why is this role still open?

This is the first question I ask — and the most important one. If a role has been open for a long time, something hasn't worked. I want to understand that before I repeat the same approach.

  • What has been tried before — which channels, which profiles?
  • Who was interviewed and rejected, and why?
  • Has the bar shifted over time, or have offers been declined?
  • Is the problem supply, internal process, or expectations?

Only once I understand the history do I build the full intake picture: candidate profile, role scope, team culture, HM style, target companies, related industries.

"List me the 5 key traits you need. Now — which leg can we remove and still have a fully functional sheep?"

This question forces prioritisation. A sheep with 5 legs is hard to find. Knowing which 4 are non-negotiable changes everything about how you search.

02 — Sourcing channels
Where to actually look
LinkedIn Recruiter
75%
Still the most active global network. Boolean strings: PHP 8+, PostgreSQL, product teams, Barcelona + EU remote. Note: for LATAM I use local platforms; for Germany, Xing.
Referrals
10%
Consistently the highest quality channel. Referred candidates arrive with context — they've heard the positives and are already engaged. Ask the HM and team for names.
GitHub
10%
Source PHP contributors with strong activity and recent commits. Find them on LinkedIn by name, or reach out via their profile or personal site.
Community groups
5%
PHP Slack channels, Discord, LI groups. Longer play — build credibility before promoting. Good for pipeline over time, not quick wins.
03 — Two ideas to stand out
Outreach that actually gets noticed
📸
Hiring manager content

Get the HM and team in a photo or short video — casual, on-brand, approachable. Post it. At FLYR I wrote ready-to-post LinkedIn content for the engineering manager so all he had to do was hit publish. A post from the HM reaches a completely different audience than one from recruitment — and candidates respond very differently to it.

🏆
PHP skills competition

Run a public PHP challenge via TestGorilla. Top 5 scoring candidates get a fast-track interview or meet-and-greet with the team. Works especially well for remote-eligible roles, generates organic interest in the PHP community, signals that Manychat hires on merit — and creates shareable content around the brand.
